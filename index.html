This online reference was created to accomodate my Fall 2013 Data Journalism, which is part of the Digital Certificate for Journalism at New York University (Continuing Studies).

The class is only five weeks long but I wanted my students to get a full grasp of the scope and possible paths. Having it online, through Github, makes it easy for me to edit and add, and is something that hopefully can be used by others not in the class.

You can read more about the authorship and technical details behind this book in the About section. Otherwise, keep scrolling down to see the Table of Contents.

Contents

Reporting

How data fits into the journalistic pursuit.

  • Don't Believe in Magic – if you're not a programmer, learn how to solve problems yourself.
  • Spreadsheet Everything – the single best tool for every kind of data collaboration.
  • Opinionated, Politically Incorrect Data – data gets dirty before it enters the database
  • Tool: Meet the Web Inspector – learning HTML/CSS is less important than learning how they provide structure to data. This will be a revision of a [tutorial I wrote for the Bastards Book of Ruby](http://ruby.bastardsbook.com/chapters/web-inspecting-html/)

Analysis

The meaning of the data.

  • Analyzing Nonsense – How to find interesting questions and leads even when the data is total bull.

Visualization

The portrayal of the data.

  • From the Brain to D3 – moving from one visualization platform to the other is just a matter of seeing the similarities in execution.
  • Text as Visualization – if lines and art aren't your thing, plain text itself can be a rich data canvas.
  • Tables and Small Multiples – sometimes, keeping your data in a spreadsheet-like format can be both the easiest and best visualization.
  • Tool: Fusion Tables – easy, spreadsheet-powered, collaborative visualizations
